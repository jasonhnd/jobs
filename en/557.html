About this occupation

General machinists use conventional machine tools—operated by hand via handles and levers, such as lathes and drill presses—to cut, grind, and otherwise shape metal stock to specified dimensions.

Machine tools fall into two broad categories: traditional "conventional" (general-purpose) machine tools, and numerically controlled (NC) machine tools operated by computer. In recent years, NC machines—which produce consistent precision and are well-suited to high-volume production—have come to account for roughly 90% of machine tool output in Japan (*1). Nevertheless, conventional machines require no programming, are highly versatile, and enable the extremely delicate finishing that only skilled craftspeople can achieve. They remain a powerful option, particularly in the production of small-lot items, prototypes, and custom-made parts.

The principal types of conventional machine tools include: the lathe (which rotates the workpiece to cut it to shape); the drill press (for drilling holes); and the milling machine (for machining flat and curved surfaces). Other types exist for specific purposes, such as the boring machine (for enlarging holes), the grinding machine (which uses an abrasive wheel rather than a cutting tool), and the gear-cutting machine (for cutting gear teeth). Except in large factories where specific machines are assigned to dedicated operators, most machinists become proficient on several types of conventional machines; depending on operational needs, handling both conventional and NC machines may be expected.

The workflow typically begins with reviewing internal work orders and CAD-generated engineering drawings, mentally simulating the sequence of operations, the machines and cutting tools required, and the machining order, and then laying out an appropriate work plan. This "job setup" is usually done independently, though for complex jobs it may involve internal consultation or team discussion.

Next, the operator mounts cutting tools and stock material in the machine, sets parameters such as cutting speed and spindle RPM, and carries out machining by operating handles and levers. Dimensional accuracy is verified partway through and again upon completion. Other work includes machine maintenance and cleaning, and—depending on the part—scribing, center-punching, bending, washing, or polishing.

General machinists are highly skilled technical specialists who support Japanese manufacturing. Many take deep pride and satisfaction in finishing parts with a level of precision that leaves no room for error.

*1 Source: Japan Machine Tool Builders' Association (JMTBA) website

Tools and equipment commonly used: lathe, drill press, milling machine and other conventional machine tools; PC; tablet; CAD software; general tools (wrenches, spanners, hammers, screwdrivers, etc.); measuring instruments (rulers, calipers, micrometers, etc.); grinding equipment (angle grinder, belt sander, sandpaper, etc.); press machine; ultrasonic cleaner; personal protective equipment (helmet, safety goggles, earplugs, safety shoes)

How to enter the field

No specific educational credentials or qualifications are required for entry. Industrial high school graduates make up a large share of the workforce; university graduates are predominantly from engineering or other science-related programs. The ratio of new-graduate to mid-career hires varies by workplace.

After joining, some large employers offer off-the-job training (Off-JT) combining classroom study with hands-on practice at in-house training facilities, but the majority of workplaces rely on one-on-one OJT with a senior mentor, working with actual production parts. Becoming proficient on a single machine tool is said to take anywhere from several months to several years. Workers continue deepening their skills by adding machine types and refining techniques. In larger workplaces, individuals who demonstrate recognized ability and experience may be appointed as group leaders responsible for directing teammates and coordinating with other departments, with a further career path into management.

The related qualification is the "Machining Technician" (機械加工技能士) skill certification administered by the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare (MHLW). The examination is categorized by machine type (conventional and NC) and grade. While not mandatory, the certification demonstrates one's skill level, may qualify holders for company-specified skill allowances, and can be advantageous in job transfers.

Note: when operating grinding machines, workers must complete the Special Training for Grinding Machine Wheel Replacement and Test Run (機械研削盤といし取替え・試運転業務特別教育) as required under the Industrial Safety and Health Act.

The role suits people who are dexterous, enjoy manufacturing, and can concentrate and persevere—the archetypal craftsperson. Strong spatial reasoning (the ability to easily understand and visualize two- and three-dimensional shapes) and the ability to plan and organize work sequences effectively are also essential.

Working conditions

Machinists work in factories ranging from large manufacturers to small family-run machine shops, located throughout Japan. Some factories lack air conditioning, which can mean discomfort in extreme heat or cold. Conventional machining requires standing for the entire operation.

Unlike NC machines—where the machining area is typically enclosed—conventional machine tools expose the operator to rotating cutting tools and spindles at close range, making a momentary lapse of attention a serious safety risk. Workers are required to wear helmets, safety goggles, and earplugs to reduce the noise generated during machining.

Men make up the large majority of the workforce. Because of the skill development involved, the predominant employment type is full-time regular employment. Salaries, hours, and other conditions are set by individual workplaces; day shifts are the norm.

The spread of CAD software in recent years has improved workflows by enabling drawings to be exchanged by email and reviewed visually on screen. However, the rise of NC machine tools is a major structural trend, and smaller operators in particular have seen a wave of business closures as workforce aging takes its toll.

Looking ahead, further advances in NC technology driven by AI may significantly change how general machinists work. Nevertheless, in the domain of craftsmanship that numerical control cannot replicate, the rarity value of true specialists is expected to endure.
